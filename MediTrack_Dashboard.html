Problem 1 — revenue fluctuation and HMO reimbursement cut
Monthly Revenue vs Patient Volume
Revenue grew steadily until January 2024 when HMO coverage was cut from 72% to 55% — reducing what the hospital collects from insured patients
HMO Coverage Impact
Before vs after January 2024 reimbursement cut

Pre-Jan 2024: HMO covered 72% of gross bill. Average collected per HMO visit: ₦18,270. Total HMO revenue: ₦133.9M from 7,329 bills.
Post-Jan 2024: HMO cut to 55% coverage. Revenue per visit unchanged (₦18,701) but hospital now carries 45% of cost vs 28% before. Estimated shortfall: ₦28.6M+ from this change alone.
Revenue by payment type: HMO/Insurance is the largest segment (₦320.9M, 39.5% of revenue). A 17-point coverage cut here has an outsized impact on hospital finances.
Action: Renegotiate HMO contracts immediately — target 65%+ coverage. Diversify toward Corporate and direct pay to reduce HMO dependency.

Key findings and recommended actions
Finding 01
HMO reimbursement cut is costing ₦28.6M+
The January 2024 HMO coverage reduction from 72% to 55% created a significant revenue gap. HMO patients represent 39.5% of all billing — any coverage change has an outsized effect on total revenue.
Renegotiate all HMO contracts before next renewal. Target 65% minimum coverage. Reduce HMO dependency by growing Corporate and direct-pay segments.
Finding 02
Mental Health and Dental no-shows destroy capacity
44.7% of Mental Health appointments and 43.4% of Dental appointments are no-shows. Each missed slot is capacity and revenue wasted. With 2,148 Mental Health and Dental no-shows combined, estimated lost revenue exceeds ₦19M.
Implement mandatory appointment deposits for Mental Health and Dental. Add WhatsApp reminders 48h and 2h before every appointment. Consider telehealth for Mental Health follow-ups.
Finding 03
Maternity staff shortage is a patient experience emergency
Wait times doubled from 37.7 to 84.3 minutes. Satisfaction crashed from 4.1 to 2.7. No-show rate nearly doubled to 30.1%. With 6,898 completed Maternity bills at an average of ₦28,610, this department's dysfunction is a major financial risk.
Hire minimum 2 additional Maternity staff this quarter. Set a 50-minute wait time target with a dashboard for daily monitoring. Offer affected patients a follow-up discount as a goodwill gesture.
Finding 04
Doctor Referral patients are the highest quality patients
Doctor Referral patients have a 32.3% active rate — 47% higher than Walk-in patients. They also average more visits per year. Yet only 28% of patient registrations come through this channel, suggesting an untapped growth lever.
Launch a formal GP Partnership Programme — host quarterly referral dinners, provide GPs with patient outcome reports, offer co-branded health packages. Target 40% of registrations from referrals within 12 months.
Finding 05
Out-of-Pocket patients need payment plan options
Out-of-Pocket patients represent 35% of billings but have the highest dropout rate. Price sensitivity is the primary reason patients miss follow-up appointments and disengage from care — not satisfaction with the service.
Introduce 3-month and 6-month payment plans for Out-of-Pocket patients on treatment programmes. Partner with 1-2 microfinance banks to offer patient financing. This directly addresses the price barrier without reducing revenue.
Finding 06
Cardiology and Maternity drive the most revenue per visit
Cardiology averages ₦40,182 per bill — 8.9x the General Practice average. Maternity averages ₦28,610. These two departments generate ₦476M — 59.8% of total hospital revenue — from just 25% of appointments.
Protect and prioritise Cardiology and Maternity capacity above all other departments. Any staffing or equipment investment here has the highest revenue return. Do not cut these budgets.
